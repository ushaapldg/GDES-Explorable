The first writing systems known today originated from three regions: Mesoamerica, present day China, and the Near East.

Prior to the development of writing systems, the concept of using symbolic figures to represent concepts is seen through the use of clay tokens in Mesopotamia, used for accounting. Clay, a material Mesopotamians had in abundance, would continue to be used as a medium for documentation for thousands of years.

Clay tokens came in different 3-dimensional shapes such as cones, spheres, cylinders, disks, and more. A clay token of a particular shape represented one kind of good, and one clay token represented one count of that good. Clay 'envelopes' encasing tokens would be used to maintain accuracy in tracking transactions and debt. Markings inscribed on the outside of the envelope authenticated its contents.

Over time, the exterior markings of the envelope were identifiable on their own, and the need for tokens to keep count dissolved. The idea of using symbols to represent numeric values eventually led to the idea of using symbols to represent sounds, a revolutionary change that allowed any concept to be documented.

The same markings from the clay envelopes evolved into Cuneiform- one of the earliest writing systems known. Cuneiform was logo-syllabic, meaning it used logograms to represent each syllable. Because the system was syllabic, it was used across many languages in Mesopotamia.

Clay remained as the common medium for writing in cuneiform because of its ability to preserve documentation over long periods. Cuneiform was written on clay tablets with a reed stylus. Reed was preferable for its solid structure and glossy skin, allowing clean inscriptions. Over time, bone and metal styli were also created, but were not as commonly used.

Hieroglyphics date back to 3100 BC. The reason for its creation is debated, with some arguing that hieroglyphics serve as a flexible system for accounting under the statehood of ancient Egyptian civilization, while others argue that they were invented to honor gods and kings through writings and inscriptions.

Hieroglyphic characters are represented using pictographs, or images representing a sound, object or idea. The origins of what influenced the use of pictographs is not well understood. Many historians believe that rock-based pictograms used by native hunting tribes influenced the creation of hieroglyphic pictographs.

Compared to cuneiform which was inscribed into clay, hieroglyphics were written on papyrus scrolls with ink using reed styli. This catered to detailed pictographs, in contrast to the markings used in cuneiform. Ink was made in red and black using soot and iron oxide. Black ink was used for the body of the text, while red was used to highlight headers and keywords in important documents.
